# 6.1 What is Insurance Fraud?

Health insurance fraud is any intentional act of providing false, misleading, or manipulated information to obtain insurance benefits.

Fraud can be committed by:

- Patients
- Hospitals
- Doctors
- Insurance agents
- Third parties

Insurance fraud increases healthcare costs and may result in claim rejection, legal action, or policy cancellation.

Example:

Submitting fake hospital bills to receive reimbursement.

Result:

The claim should be investigated before approval.

---

# 6.2 Common Types of Fraud

Common examples include:

- Fake hospital bills
- Altered invoices
- Duplicate claims
- Inflated treatment costs
- Billing for services not provided
- Fake diagnostic reports
- Identity fraud
- False hospitalization
- Forged medical certificates

Example:

A claimant submits the same hospital bill to two different insurance companies.

Result:

This may be treated as a duplicate claim and investigated for fraud.

---

# 6.3 Fraud Indicators

The following situations may indicate possible fraud and should be reviewed carefully:

- Mismatch in patient names across documents.
- Different admission dates in different records.
- Unusually high medical charges compared to similar treatments.
- Duplicate bills or invoices.
- Missing hospital stamp or doctor's signature.
- Altered bill amounts.
- Repeated claims for the same treatment.
- Missing supporting medical records.
- Handwritten changes on printed bills without authorization.

Example:

Hospital Bill:
₹1,20,000

Itemized Bill Total:
₹95,000

Result:

The mismatch should be investigated before claim approval.

---

# 6.4 Hospital Fraud

Fraud may also be committed by hospitals.

Examples include:

- Billing for treatments that were never provided.
- Charging for medicines not administered.
- Inflating room rent or ICU charges.
- Creating fake hospitalization records.
- Charging duplicate investigation fees.

Example:

A hospital bills for three MRI scans, but only one scan was actually performed.

Result:

The claim should be reviewed for possible hospital fraud.

---

# 6.5 Patient Fraud

Patients may intentionally submit false information to obtain insurance benefits.

Examples include:

- Submitting forged medical documents.
- Concealing previous claims.
- Using another person's insurance policy.
- Submitting duplicate reimbursement claims.
- Altering medical bills.

Example:

A patient changes the hospital bill amount from ₹45,000 to ₹95,000 before submission.

Result:

The claim should be flagged for fraud investigation.

---

# 6.6 Fraud Detection Rules

Before approving any claim, perform the following checks:

- Verify that the patient's name is consistent across all documents.
- Confirm that admission and discharge dates match.
- Compare the final bill with the itemized bill.
- Check whether the claimed treatment matches the diagnosis.
- Verify that the hospital is genuine and authorized.
- Look for duplicate claims submitted previously.
- Check for altered or tampered documents.
- Ensure that all mandatory documents are available.

Decision Guidelines:

- If no suspicious indicators are found, continue claim processing.
- If minor inconsistencies are found, request clarification or additional documents.
- If strong fraud indicators are detected, refer the claim for fraud investigation before making a final decision.

---

# 6.7 Fraud Investigation Process

If a claim is flagged for possible fraud, the insurer should follow a structured investigation process.

Typical steps include:

1. Review all submitted documents.
2. Verify hospital records.
3. Contact the treating doctor if necessary.
4. Compare medical records with billing records.
5. Verify patient identity.
6. Check previous claim history.
7. Prepare an investigation report.
8. Make the final claim decision.

The investigation should be completed before approving or rejecting the claim.
