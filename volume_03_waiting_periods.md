# 3.1 What is a Waiting Period?

A waiting period is the amount of time an insured person must wait before certain benefits become payable under the health insurance policy.

Even if the policy is active, some treatments cannot be claimed until the waiting period has been completed.

Waiting periods help insurers prevent immediate claims after purchasing a policy.

Example:

Policy Purchase Date:
1 January 2025

Hospitalization Date:
15 January 2025

If the treatment is subject to a waiting period, the claim may not be payable.

---

# 3.2 Initial Waiting Period

Most health insurance policies have an initial waiting period of 30 days from the policy start date.

During this period, illnesses are generally not covered.

Exception:

Accidental injuries are usually covered from the first day of the policy.

Example:

Policy Start:
1 January 2025

Hospitalization:
20 January 2025

Reason:
Fever

Result:

Claim is generally not payable because the 30-day waiting period is not completed.

Example:

Policy Start:
1 January 2025

Hospitalization:
20 January 2025

Reason:
Road Accident

Result:

The claim may be payable because accidental hospitalization is normally covered immediately.

---

# 3.3 Pre-existing Disease Waiting Period

A pre-existing disease (PED) is any illness, injury, or medical condition that existed before purchasing the health insurance policy.

Most insurers apply a waiting period before claims related to pre-existing diseases become payable.

The waiting period commonly ranges from 2 to 4 years, depending on the policy.

Examples of pre-existing diseases include:

- Diabetes
- Hypertension
- Asthma
- Thyroid disorders
- Heart disease

Example:

Policy Purchase Date:
1 January 2025

Hospitalization:
10 March 2026

Reason:
Diabetes-related complications

Policy Waiting Period:
3 years

Result:

The claim may not be payable because the waiting period has not been completed.

---

# 3.4 Specific Disease Waiting Period

Some illnesses have their own waiting periods even if they are not pre-existing.

The waiting period is generally between 1 and 2 years depending on the insurer.

Examples include:

- Hernia
- Cataract
- Kidney stones
- Varicose veins
- Joint replacement
- Gall bladder stones

Example:

Policy Start:
1 January 2025

Hospitalization:
15 August 2025

Reason:
Cataract Surgery

Specific Waiting Period:
2 years

Result:

The claim may not be payable because the required waiting period has not been completed.

---

# 3.5 Maternity Waiting Period

Most health insurance policies apply a waiting period before maternity-related expenses become eligible.

The waiting period commonly ranges from 9 months to 4 years.

Coverage may include:

- Normal delivery
- Caesarean delivery
- Pregnancy-related hospitalization
- Newborn baby coverage (if specified in the policy)

Example:

Policy Purchase:
January 2025

Delivery:
July 2025

Waiting Period:
24 months

Result:

The maternity claim may not be payable because the waiting period has not been completed.

---

# 3.6 Accident Cases

Most health insurance policies do not apply the initial waiting period to accidental injuries.

If hospitalization occurs due to an accident after the policy becomes active, eligible expenses are generally covered according to the policy terms.

Examples of accidental injuries include:

- Road traffic accidents
- Falls
- Burns
- Fractures
- Head injuries caused by accidents

Example:

Policy Start:
1 January 2025

Accident Date:
5 January 2025

Hospitalization:
5 January 2025

Result:

The claim may be payable because accidental hospitalization is generally covered immediately.

---

# 3.7 Waiting Period Decision Rules

Before approving a claim, verify the following:

- Is the policy active?
- What is the policy start date?
- What is the hospitalization date?
- Is the illness pre-existing?
- Does the disease have a specific waiting period?
- Is the claim related to maternity?
- Is the hospitalization due to an accident?

Decision Guidelines:

- If the required waiting period is completed, continue claim assessment.
- If the waiting period is not completed, the claim may be rejected or kept pending according to policy terms.
- Accident-related hospitalization is generally exempt from the initial waiting period.
